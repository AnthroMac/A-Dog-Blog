Burial Ceremonies From Around The World

When I think of a burial, I picture a body being placed in the ground, however, ceremonies around the world are diverse and traditions even in the Pacific Northwest do not reflect my notions of burials. Here are four ceremonies from different parts of the world. See 10 here.

The four photos I have chosen to represent burials around the world are:

Top left: Tibet “sky Burial”

The deceased is dismembered by a “rogyapa”, and left outside to be consumed by nature. The body of the deceased is seen as just a shell for the person who once lived in there. By placing the bodies outside it provides sustenance to the birds who are the primary consumers of its flesh.

Top Right: Haida - Pacific NorthWest:

The Body of a chief, shaman, or warrior was crushed with clubs to fit into a small wooden box. It was then fitted to the top of a totem pole in front of the longhouse of the man’s tribe where the various icons of the totem acted as guardians for the spirits’ journey to the next world.

Bottom Left: Bali “Fire Burials”

On the Hindu Isle of Bali, fire is considered a vehicle to get to the next life. The deceased bodies are unearthed, cleaned, and stacked on an elaborate float, with beautiful decorations and flowers, the float is walked through the village then lit on fire.

Bottom Right: Kirbati, Oceana (small Island off of Australia, not pictured on the map)

Christian burials are now widespread among the Kiribati people, however traditionally, bodies would be exhumed months after a burial in order to have the skull removed, oiled, polished and offered tobacco and food. The skulls would be kept on display while the rest of the body returned to earth. Kiribati people believe(d) in the native god Nakaa who would welcome the spirits of the deceased.
